Shadokean proverbs for researchers :

“Pourquoi faire simple quand on peut faire compliqué ?”
(Why do it the easy way when you can do it the hard way ?)

“Ce n’est qu’en essayant continuellement que l’on finit par réussir…. En d’autres termes… Plus ça rate et plus on a de chances que ça marche…”
(When one tries continuously, one ends up succeeding. Thus, the more one fails, the greater the chance that it will work)

“S’il n’y a pas de solution. C’est qu’il n’y a pas de problème.”
(If there is no solution, it is because there is no problem)

“Quand on ne sait pas où on va, il faut y aller…. et le plus vite possible”
(When you don’t know where to go, you should go there .. and as quick as possible)

merci Jacques Rouxel

Research Interests

Soft Matter Physics (polymers, grains, active matter)
Soft Matter is usually split in two main group: particles in equilibrium with a thermal bath (polymers, colloids) and athermal systems such as granular matter. A third group based on the concept of Active Particle recently appears. In these out-of-equilibrium systems, the energy is injected at the scale of the particles, in contrast to classical dissipative structures where energy is provided through boundaries (e.g., Rayleigh-Bénard convection). We have studied various topics such as the influence of out-of-equilibrium chain conformations on the macroscopic properties of polymer or the rheology of granular matter from a microscopic approach.

Mechanics of Slender Structures (sheets, rods, ribbons, shells and balloons)
Soft elastic structures, i.e., involving at least one very small dimension, are prone to undergo mechanical instability. By confining a thin sheet in a empty ball, in a curtain-shape, twisting a ribbon or gluing a flexible film on top of a deformable foundation, you can observe the growth of various patterns such as, regular wrinkles or a network of stress focusing singularities. Our research aims to understand the formation of these complex patterns from a combination of geometry and energy minimisation coupled to nonlinearities.

Biomechanics (lizards, bees, hummingbirs)
In this research activity, we apply our knowledge in elasticity and fluid flow to understand the structure and function of biological organisms. Our works aim to contribute to answer fundamental questions in the living world, such as: How do mechanics determine the feeding and drinking strategy of animals ? How do elastic instability explain the various shapes of plants ?
